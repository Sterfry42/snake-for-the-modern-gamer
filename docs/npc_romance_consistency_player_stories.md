# NPC/Romance Consistency Player Stories

This pass treats NPCs as people with memory, faction pressure, fear, pride, hunger, and public lives.

## Design Commitments

- Beat text and spoken text are separate pages.
- NPCs should respond in their own voice before mechanical relationship outcomes appear.
- A choice should name itself in the flow: the player chose `Pineapple`, `Own the Harm`, `Stand With Them`, etc.
- Neutral is rare. Most intimacy scenes should have two emotionally readable positive routes and one risky route, or one excellent route, one acceptable route, and one bad route.
- Authored romance scenes own their outcome tiers. A generic tag sorter may help older fallback content, but it should not overwrite a designed scene.
- Romance cannot erase public harm. If the player hurts a faction or kills a townsperson, affection should become complicated, not blind.
- Personality should appear before, during, and after a choice. A regal NPC should not merely like bravery; they should talk about dignity, witness, and standing properly. A hungry NPC should read care through food, warmth, and appetite.
- Quests should sound like requests from someone with stakes, not UI copy.

## Story 1: Cyrene, First Talk To First Flirt

Player fantasy:
The player sees Cyrene and wants her to be more than a generated portrait. They press talk, expecting one more throwaway line, but the game gives them a beat first.

Flow:

1. The player talks outside romance.
2. The UI shows a beat page: Cyrene turns the moment over like a wet letter.
3. The player presses Listen.
4. Cyrene speaks in her own voice.
5. The player opens Romance.
6. Flirt starts with a setup, not instant scoring.
7. Cyrene asks a specific question: a topping at a late snack stall.
8. The player chooses Pineapple.
9. The next page says: You chose Pineapple.
10. Cyrene reacts specifically to pineapple.
11. Cyrene gets one more line explaining why that answer worked for her.
12. Only after that does the relationship result appear.

Why it matters:
The player should not wonder which quote was pineapple. The scene should hold the player by the hand through cause and effect without becoming mechanical.

## Story 2: Cyrene And The Faction Aftershock

Player fantasy:
The player shot someone in town, then goes to flirt with Cyrene. The old system allowed affection to float separately from fear and faction reality.

Flow:

1. The player starts a faction fight.
2. Town rumors and actor memory remember the violence.
3. Cyrene has affection, but the street is still stained.
4. Talk or romance surfaces the faction-aftershock scene.
5. Cyrene asks: what do you expect me to do with affection when your actions hurt a faction I know?
6. The player can own the harm, promise repair, or blame the faction.
7. Owning the harm is loved because it gives affection a truthful place to stand.
8. Promising repair is liked because it is not absolution yet.
9. Blaming the faction is hated because it asks romance to become denial.

Why it matters:
This is the “I still love you, but the town is after you” contradiction turned into drama instead of ignored state.

## Story 3: The Bear Date Is About Agency

Player fantasy:
The player wants a wild Phoenix Wright-ish date with a bear, but they also want the NPC to have pride.

Flow:

1. Date starts in a tavern.
2. The NPC establishes their personality through the room, food, exits, dignity, or contracts.
3. A bear crashes through the wall.
4. The player can stand with them, shield them, or run.
5. Standing with them is loved because it respects agency.
6. Shielding them is disliked for proud personalities because it makes them smaller.
7. Running is hated because it abandons them.

Why it matters:
Protection is not always liked. The reason depends on the person.

## Story 4: The Shrine Date Tests Boundaries

Player fantasy:
The player wants intimacy that is not just flirting. They want to learn what the NPC considers sacred or private.

Flow:

1. Date reaches an abandoned shrine.
2. The NPC frames the shrine through personality.
3. The player sees three offerings: coin, flower, apology note.
4. Taking the flower is loved: tenderness without spectacle.
5. Coin is liked: practical respect.
6. Reading the apology note is hated: sealed pain is not public loot.

Why it matters:
The system should teach the player what a person values. Curiosity can be charming in one scene and invasive in another.

## Story 5: Public Future Question

Player fantasy:
The player is teased by a child asking if the relationship is serious, doomed, or weird.

Flow:

1. The NPC reacts to being perceived.
2. The player can say Serious, Doomed, or Just Weird.
3. Serious is loved because it names commitment publicly.
4. Doomed is liked because it has wit but may dodge.
5. Just Weird is disliked because it shrinks the relationship in public.

Why it matters:
Public affection should not be a flat tag. Some NPCs like the spectacle; some like the courage; some dislike being minimized.

## Story 6: Quest Giver As Person

Player fantasy:
The player meets a quest giver and does not want to hear “There is work to be done.”

Flow:

1. Quest popup opens.
2. The quest giver speaks a request.
3. The line names stakes: hunger, debt, animals, maze pride, or public harm.
4. The player accepts because the request has a person behind it.

Why it matters:
A quest is a relationship seed. Even one-off quest text should feel like someone needs something.

## Story 7: Marriage After Damage

Player fantasy:
The player loves an NPC, proposes, completes the bouquet quest, but has also created faction problems.

Flow:

1. Proposal succeeds only if affection, trust, resentment, jealousy, and existing commitments allow it.
2. If faction harm exists, spouse dialogue should not become blind joy.
3. The spouse should say: my vow does not include pretending your enemies are imaginary.
4. Marriage can proceed, but public memory remains.

Why it matters:
Marriage is not a state reset. It is a new lens on old consequences.

## Story 8: Child And Abandonment

Player fantasy:
The player marries, has a child, then abandons the NPC.

Flow:

1. Child arrives as a relationship memory and social fact.
2. Neglect ticks upward after room changes.
3. The spouse does not just lose affection; they reference the family unit.
4. Hungry spouse talks about cold meals and empty places.
5. Regal spouse talks about broken duties.
6. Sharp spouse talks about undisclosed abandonment terms.
7. Poetic spouse talks about a cradle learning silence.
8. Deadpan spouse says the household has become statistically one parent short.

Why it matters:
Abandonment should be emotionally legible and personality-specific.

## Story 9: Hostility After Betrayal

Player fantasy:
The player betrays a lover, then tries to talk.

Flow:

1. The NPC no longer uses soft romance barks.
2. Beat page appears: the old warmth has learned to carry a blade.
3. The NPC speaks from hurt, not generic hostility.
4. If combat starts, that hostility is consistent with relationship state.

Why it matters:
Romance failure should not feel like a menu switched to enemy mode. It should feel like this person broke.

## Story 10: Ask Around In A Living Town

Player fantasy:
The player asks around after a shop theft.

Flow:

1. The NPC gives a beat: checking exits, folding receipt, touching badge.
2. They speak about the rumor through personality.
3. Shopkeeper mentions prices and shelf space.
4. Guard mentions paperwork and public order.
5. Thief mentions doors and names as currency.
6. Romance candidate mentions how public consequences enter private affection.

Why it matters:
Rumors are not lore dumps. They are social weather.

## Story 11: Ask Personally

Player fantasy:
The player earns enough trust to ask personally.

Flow:

1. The UI shows only a beat page first.
2. The NPC reveals a wound, insecurity, longing, contradiction, or social tie.
3. The known fact appears on its own page.
4. The spoken line remains the NPC’s line, not a system label.

Why it matters:
The player should feel like they were trusted, not like they unlocked a database field.

## Story 12: Enemy Clarity

Player fantasy:
The player is unsure if snake enemies can kill them or be killed.

Flow:

1. Enemy contact and projectiles must visibly affect health unless invulnerability is active.
2. Player attacks must visibly remove or damage enemies when valid.
3. If a cheat prevents death, the game should surface that state somewhere debug-visible.
4. If enemy combat is broken, it should be treated as a separate correctness bug from dialogue.

Why it matters:
Drama depends on danger. Romance stakes work better when the world can actually hurt you.
